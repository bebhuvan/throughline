When you pick up a bag of rice or a carton of eggs at your local supermarket, the price on the sticker feels like a local fact. But it isn't. That number is the endpoint of a chain of events that stretches across oceans, through chokepoints, and into the geopolitics of energy and fertilizer.

In the past two years, a series of disruptions — some sudden, some slow-building — have pushed global food prices into territory that is quietly punishing the world's most vulnerable populations.

## The fertilizer bottleneck

Most people understand that when oil prices rise, transport costs go up, and food gets more expensive. That is the first-order effect.

But the second-order effect is less visible and more consequential: **fertilizer**.

Natural gas is the primary feedstock for nitrogen-based fertilizers, which account for roughly half of the world's food production capacity. When gas prices spike — whether from sanctions, supply disruptions, or geopolitical conflict — the cost of fertilizer follows within weeks.

The price of fertilizer doesn't just affect industrial farming. It reshapes the economics of subsistence agriculture in South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and parts of Latin America — where smallholder farmers often cannot afford to buy fertilizer at all when prices double.

<Callout type="insight" title="The fertilizer-food lag">
  A spike in fertilizer prices takes 3 to 6 months to show up in food prices. The lag creates a false sense of calm — by the time consumers notice, the damage is already locked in for the next growing season.
</Callout>

## Who pays the steepest price?

Food price inflation is not distributed equally. In wealthier countries, food accounts for roughly 10–15% of household spending. In low-income countries, that figure is often 40–60%.

This means a 20% increase in food costs is an inconvenience in Berlin but a crisis in Lagos. The asymmetry is stark, and it is one of the most underappreciated dimensions of global supply chain disruptions.

<KeyInsight>
  A disruption at a single chokepoint — a strait, a pipeline, a port — can alter the food security of millions of people who have never heard of it and have no power to respond to it.
</KeyInsight>

What makes the current moment especially dangerous is not any single disruption but the compounding effect of several happening in sequence.

## The road ahead

There are no quick fixes. The interconnectedness that makes global food systems efficient also makes them fragile. Diversifying fertilizer supply chains, investing in regional food production, and building strategic food reserves are long-term projects — not emergency measures.

But understanding the chain of causation — from a geopolitical event thousands of miles away to the price of bread at your local store — is the first step toward demanding better policy responses.

The next time you notice food prices rising, look beyond the sticker. The explanation is rarely local.
